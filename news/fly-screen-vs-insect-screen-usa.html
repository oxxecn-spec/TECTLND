Fly Screens vs Insect Screens: What US Homeowners Need to Know

May 2, 2026 6 min read Fly Screen, Insect Screen

When you shop for window and door screens, you’ll see two terms: fly screen and insect screen (often called mosquito screen). Many assume they’re the same, but there are critical differences that affect how well they protect your home – especially in the United States.

What Is a Fly Screen?

A fly screen is designed to keep out larger insects – houseflies, bluebottles, wasps, moths. Mesh openings are 1.2‑1.5 mm (about 16‑18 holes per inch). It offers excellent airflow and visibility but does not block mosquitoes or tiny biting midges.

What Is an Insect Screen?

An insect screen (mosquito screen) uses finer mesh – 0.6‑0.8 mm openings (20‑22 holes per inch). It blocks mosquitoes, gnats, midges, and sandflies. It also reduces dust and pollen. Airflow is slightly reduced but still very good (75‑85%).

Which One for US Regions?

  • Florida, Gulf Coast, Texas: High mosquito pressure. Use insect screen (20‑22 mesh), preferably 304 stainless steel for durability in humidity.
  • California, Southwest: Occasional mosquitoes but also flies. Insect screen is the safe choice.
  • Northeast & Midwest: Seasonal mosquitoes. Insect screen recommended.
  • Pacific Northwest: Few mosquitoes but midges. Fine mesh insect screen needed.

Mesh Material Matters

Polyester (PET): good for most windows. Fibreglass: cheap but short‑lived – avoid. 304 stainless steel: best for doors, pets, and coastal homes.
