Fly Screen vs Insect Screen: Which One Do You Need?

16 May 2026 6 min read Fly Screen, Insect Screen

When shopping for window and door screens, you'll often see two terms: fly screen and insect screen (or mosquito screen). Many people assume they are the same, but there are important differences that affect how well they protect your home.

In this guide, we’ll compare fly screen vs insect screen side by side – mesh sizes, airflow, light transmission, durability, and which one is right for each room.

What is a Fly Screen?

A fly screen is designed primarily to keep out larger flying insects – houseflies, bluebottles, wasps, and moths. The mesh holes are typically 1.2mm to 1.5mm (about 16‑18 holes per inch). This size stops flies but allows good airflow and visibility. Fly screens are common in kitchens, restaurants, and areas where flies are the main nuisance.

What is an Insect Screen?

An insect screen (often called mosquito screen or midge screen) uses much finer mesh – usually 0.6mm to 0.8mm openings (20‑22 holes per inch). This blocks not only flies but also mosquitoes, gnats, midges, and tiny biting insects. It also prevents dust and pollen from entering. However, finer mesh reduces airflow slightly compared to a fly screen.

Which One Should You Choose?

Choose a fly screen if: you live in an area with few mosquitoes but have a problem with houseflies. Also suitable for kitchens where you need maximum airflow and easy cleaning.

Choose an insect screen if: mosquitoes, midges, or sandflies are present – which is true for most of the world. Also ideal for bedrooms, nurseries, and any space where you sleep or spend time at dusk/dawn when mosquitoes are active.

For most residential applications, an insect protection screen is the better investment. It keeps out both flies and mosquitoes, and the slight reduction in airflow is rarely noticeable.

Mesh Material Matters: Polyester vs Fibreglass vs Stainless Steel

Beyond mesh size, the material affects durability and performance:

  • Polyester – Lightweight, flexible, UV‑resistant, and less prone to fraying. Good for standard insect screens.
  • Fibreglass – Inexpensive but can fray and tear more easily; often used in cheap fly screens.
  • 304 Stainless Steel – Extremely durable, pet‑proof, rust‑resistant. Best for high‑traffic doors, coastal homes, and areas with aggressive insects.

Application Examples

  • Kitchen window: A fly screen (16‑18 mesh) or standard insect screen works well. If you also open windows at night, go for insect screen.
  • Bedroom window: Definitely an insect screen (20‑22 mesh) to keep mosquitoes away while you sleep.
  • Patio door (high‑traffic): A folding insect screen door with 304 stainless steel mesh for durability and full mosquito protection.
  • Restaurant terrace: Crystal‑clear PVC panels (transparent insect barrier) combined with bug‑proof mesh.

Conclusion

The choice between a fly screen and an insect screen comes down to the insects in your area. If mosquitoes are a concern – and they almost always are – invest in a high‑quality insect screen. It offers superior protection against a wider range of pests while still providing good airflow.
