Mosquito Net Screen – Complete Guide for Homeowners

2 May 2026 8 min read Mosquito Net Screen, Insect Protection

A mosquito net screen (often called an insect screen or fly mesh) is one of the simplest and most effective ways to keep bugs out while letting fresh air in. But with so many types – retractable, fixed, magnetic, folding – how do you choose? This guide covers everything you need to know about mosquito net screens for windows and doors.

What is a Mosquito Net Screen?

A mosquito net screen is a fine mesh mounted over a window or door opening. It blocks mosquitoes, flies, midges, and other flying insects while allowing air, light, and sound to pass through. Modern screens are made from materials like PET (polyester), fibreglass, or 304 stainless steel, and can be installed in many ways – from simple adhesive tapes to professional retractable systems.

Types of Mosquito Net Screens

1. Fixed Frame Screens

These are permanently attached to the window or door frame. They are the most affordable and durable option. However, they cannot be removed easily and may obstruct the view when the window is closed. Ideal for windows that are rarely opened.

2. Retractable Screens

A window mosquito screen that rolls up into a slim cassette when not in use. When you open the window, you pull the screen down; when you close the window, the screen rolls away. Perfect for homes where you want an unobstructed view. Available for windows and doors.

3. Magnetic Screens

These consist of two mesh panels with magnetic strips along the vertical split. They are ideal for doors – you can walk through and the magnets automatically close behind you. Some versions use adhesive velcro for mounting, making them rental‑friendly.

4. Folding (Concertina) Screens

For large openings like patio doors, a folding screen door (pleated mesh) slides sideways and folds into a compact stack. It provides a wide opening without a central post. Folding screens are often used for French doors or wide sliding doors.

5. Adhesive (Velcro) Screens

The most rental‑friendly option. Hook‑and‑loop tape is stuck to the window frame, and the mesh is pressed onto it. No drilling, no tools. Can be removed and reattached many times. Best for casement or awning windows.

Choosing the Right Mesh Material

  • PET (Polyester): Lightweight, UV‑resistant, soft to touch. Good for most residential applications. Not recommended for aggressive pets.
  • Fibreglass: Cheap but frays easily. Not long‑lasting.
  • 304 Stainless Steel: Extremely strong, pet‑proof, rust‑resistant. Best for high‑traffic doors, coastal areas, and homes with dogs/cats.

Where Should You Install a Mosquito Net Screen?

  • Bedrooms: Essential for mosquito‑free sleep. Use fine mesh (20‑22 holes/inch).
  • Kitchens: Use easy‑clean PVC folding panels or adhesive screens that can be removed for cleaning.
  • Living rooms / patios: Choose retractable or folding screens that preserve the view.
  • Garages / workshops: Heavy‑duty 304 stainless steel folding screens.

DIY vs Professional Installation

Adhesive and magnetic screens can be installed by anyone in minutes. Retractable and folding systems often require basic tools (screwdriver, spirit level) but come with detailed instructions. For large or complex openings, professional installation is recommended, though many confident DIYers manage successfully.

  • Magnetic curtain (door): $20‑50
  • Fixed frame screen (custom): $30‑100
  • Retractable window screen (custom): $50‑200
  • Folding door screen (custom, stainless mesh): $200‑500

Maintenance Tips

Clean your screens twice a year with a soft brush or vacuum cleaner. For stubborn dirt, use mild soap and water. Do not use high‑pressure water as it can stretch the mesh. Inspect for holes and repair small tears with mesh repair tape.
